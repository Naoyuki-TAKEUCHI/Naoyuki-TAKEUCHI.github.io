Research

Martian nightside plasma, from structure to source.

My work combines Mars Express/MARSIS radar observations with MAVEN plasma and neutral/ion measurements to understand how nightside plasma is produced, transported, and structured.

Theme 01

Nightside ionosphere

MARSIS AIS observations reveal where the Martian nightside ionosphere is detectable and how peak electron density varies globally.

  • Long-term mapping of detection rates and peak densities.
  • Dependence on solar zenith angle, local time, season, and altitude.
  • Comparison with crustal magnetic field strength and geometry.
B

Crustal magnetic fields

Strong crustal fields can create localized plasma structures and modify access paths for precipitating electrons.

E

Solar wind electric field

+E and −E hemispheres provide a practical way to test draping-controlled access and asymmetries in nightside plasma.

P

Dynamic pressure

Solar wind compression may alter plasma transport and electron precipitation, especially in weak crustal-field regions.

Theme 02

Aurora–ionosphere coupling

The next research step is to connect nightside ionospheric detections with electron precipitation and ultraviolet auroral observations.

Electron precipitation

Energetic electrons can ionize the nightside atmosphere and may explain localized or enhanced ionospheric signatures.

Observational test

Comparing MARSIS, MAVEN plasma instruments, and UV auroral observations can distinguish particle impact from transport-dominated sources.

Theme 03

Nightside ion composition

MAVEN/NGIMS enables species-resolved tests of source mechanisms. O⁺, O₂⁺, CO₂⁺, and NO⁺ respond differently to chemistry, transport, and particle precipitation.

  • Altitude profiles for major ion species.
  • Dependence on solar wind and crustal-field conditions.
  • Constraints on electron-impact ionization and dayside-to-nightside transport.
